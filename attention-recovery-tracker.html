Meditate

Focused attention — the core attention rep

5:00
ready
how to do it
1
Sit comfortably
Upright, spine relatively straight. Hands resting on thighs. Eyes closed or soft gaze downward.
2
Choose your anchor
The breath at the nostrils — the coolness of air coming in, warmth going out. Pick one spot and stay there.
3
Just observe
Don't control your breath. Don't breathe in any special way. Just notice the natural rhythm.
4
When you drift — return
Mind wanders? Notice it, then gently return to the breath. No judgment. Each return is one attention rep.

The science

Peer-reviewed evidence behind every habit

what's happening
Meta-analysis — 98,000 people
Heavy short-form video use is linked to poorer attention span and reduced inhibitory control. The brain adapts to rapid-fire dopamine, making sustained tasks progressively harder.
Narrative review 2019–2025
Heavy use is associated with shorter attention spans, poorer academic performance, and abnormal white matter in areas of the brain linked to behavioral control.
recovery — by timeline
Cyberpsychology, Behavior & Social Networking — 2022
One week off social media produced significant improvements in wellbeing, reduced depression and reduced anxiety.
Timeline: 1 week
PNAS Nexus — 467 participants
14-day smartphone detox reversed the equivalent of 10 years of age-related cognitive decline in sustained attention. Effect on depression exceeded antidepressants.
Timeline: 2 weeks
ScienceDirect — 1,447 participants across 12 studies
Focused attention (FA) meditation consistently improved sustained attention, reduced perceived stress, and increased focused attention. Meditators showed faster reactions and lower error rates.
Timeline: 21 days – 3 months
Brain imaging studies — former heavy users
Former heavy social media users showed increased gray matter density in the prefrontal cortex and reduced amygdala reactivity after 8 weeks of daily meditation practice.
Timeline: 8 weeks
Long-term detox study
90-day groups showed significant restoration of gray matter density. 180-day participants showed near-complete reversal of structural brain changes linked to heavy social media use.
Timeline: 3–6 months
